def sort(real, fake, mean, sd, sigma):
    testGoodTrue = 0
    testBadTrue = 0
    testGoodBad = 0
    testBadBad = 0
    for point in real:
        if point >= mean + sd*sigma or point <= mean -sd*sigma: # thrown out good data
             testGoodBad += 1
        else:
            testGoodTrue += 1
    for point in fake:
        if point >= mean + sd*sigma or point <= mean -sd*sigma: # thrown out fake data
            testBadBad += 1
        else:
            testBadTrue += 1
    return[testGoodTrue, testGoodBad, testBadTrue, testBadBad]

1) What is the question in words?¶

How many sigma away from the mean is the measurement is the potential asteroid measurement that I took?

2) What is the question in math?¶

Since we have 2 gaussian distributions, both with $\sigma$ of 1, we can replace this with a Rayleigh with a sigma of 1. Given this, we want to find the probability that our measurement, or a measurement even further from the mean was generated by Rayleigh distribution of background noise. Thus, we would find that the equation is $\int_{-\infty}^{a}xe^{\frac{-x^2}{2}}dx$, where $a$ is our measured value.

    sum_poisson = np.convolve(sum_poisson, base_poisson)

$\int_{0}^{6800}{PMF(x)dx}$ where $PMF(x)$ is the probability mass function for $x$ number of events, and $dx$ is one event. Thus, this is the summation from 0 to 6800 of the probability of getting that meassurement. Since $PMF(x)$ is effectively a Poisson distribution, we can rewrite the integral as $\int_{0}^{6800}{\frac{\lambda^ke^{-k}}{k!}dx}$. Since $\lambda=1$, we can write the integral as $\int_{0}^{6800}{\frac{e^{-k}}{k!}dx}$. This will give the probability of our reading occuring given the background distribution.
